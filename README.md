# Visual Cryptography for Secret Image Sharing

This is a Computer Networks and Security (CNS) mini-project that demonstrates a **2-out-of-2 Visual Cryptography** scheme.

## 📌 Problem Statement
In secure communication, transmitting sensitive visual data over untrusted channels poses a risk of interception. Traditional encryption algorithms require computational power to decrypt data. The problem addressed in this project is to securely share a secret image such that the encrypted shares reveal absolutely no information when viewed individually, and can be decrypted purely by the human visual system (or simulated digital overlay) without any computational decryption algorithms.

## 🎯 Objective
- To implement a 2-out-of-2 visual cryptography algorithm.
- To demonstrate how an image can be split into two random-noise shares.
- To show that only the authorized combination (overlaying both shares) reveals the original secret.

## ⚙️ Architecture & Workflow
1. **Input**: User uploads a grayscale or black-and-white image.
2. **Binarization**: The image is thresholded into strict black (`0`) and white (`255`) pixels using OpenCV (Otsu's method).
3. **Encryption (Share Generation)**:
   - The algorithm uses a **2x2 pixel expansion**.
   - For every **White** pixel, Share 1 and Share 2 receive the *identical* random pattern (2 white, 2 black sub-pixels).
   - For every **Black** pixel, Share 1 receives a random pattern, and Share 2 receives the *inverted* pattern.
4. **Decryption (Reconstruction)**:
   - The two shares are overlaid using a Bitwise AND operation, which perfectly simulates stacking two physical transparent sheets. 
   - Light passes only where both sheets are transparent (white).

## 🧠 Viva Questions & Answers

**Q1: What is Visual Cryptography?**
**A1:** It is a cryptographic technique which allows visual information to be encrypted in such a way that decryption becomes a mechanical operation (overlaying transparencies) that does not require a computer.

**Q2: Why does the reconstructed image look slightly darker or lower resolution?**
**A2:** This is due to the **pixel expansion** factor. In our 2x2 scheme, a single white pixel from the original image becomes a block of 2 white and 2 black pixels in the reconstructed image. Thus, "white" areas appear as 50% grey, resulting in a loss of contrast.

**Q3: Can someone crack the secret image if they only have Share 1?**
**A3:** No. The algorithm guarantees unconditional security. Share 1 is generated by picking a completely random pattern for every pixel. It acts as a one-time pad. Without Share 2, Share 1 provides mathematically zero information about the original image.

**Q4: Why do we convert the image to binary (black and white) first?**
**A4:** Standard visual cryptography relies on the properties of light transmission (transparent or opaque). It cannot naturally encrypt continuous-tone grayscale or color images without halftone preprocessing, so strict binary binarization is required as the first step.

**Q5: What operation is used to combine the two shares digitally?**
**A5:** We use a **Bitwise AND** operation. In our code, White is 255 (transparent) and Black is 0 (opaque). When stacking physical transparencies, light only passes if BOTH sheets are transparent. This maps perfectly to the logical AND operation (`255 & 255 = 255`, `0 & 255 = 0`).
